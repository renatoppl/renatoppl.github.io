Lights Out on Surfaces

Solvability of a Linear Switching Game over \(\mathbb{F}_2\)
Abstract

Lights Out is a switch-setting puzzle that is, at heart, a system of linear equations over the two-element field \(\mathbb{F}_2\). We set up that dictionary, then play the game on six surfaces obtained by gluing the edges of a square—the square, cylinder, torus, Möbius strip, Klein bottle, and projective plane. A two-line linear-algebra argument (Sutner's theorem) shows that on every one of them the all-on board can always be reached from the all-off board, for every size. Finally we replace the cross-shaped button with an arbitrary stamp and characterize solvability through two parity invariants of the toggle matrix; on the boundaryless surfaces this collapses to a clean statement about the parity of the stamp size.

1The game as linear algebra over \(\mathbb{F}_2\)

A Lights Out board is a finite set \(V\) of cells; each cell carries a light (on/off) and a button. Pressing the button at a cell \(v\) toggles the light at \(v\) and the lights at all cells adjacent to \(v\)—the closed neighborhood \(N[v]=\{v\}\cup\{u:u\sim v\}\). The original commercial puzzle (Tiger Electronics, 1995) is the \(5\times5\) grid in which a press flips a plus-shaped patch of five lights:

Work over \(\mathbb{F}_2=\{0,1\}\), writing \(1\) for “on” and \(0\) for “off.” Two observations make the puzzle linear. First, toggling is its own inverse, so pressing a button twice is the same as not pressing it; a strategy is therefore just a subset of buttons, recorded as a vector \(x\in\mathbb{F}_2^{V}\). Second, the toggles add modulo \(2\) and commute, so the order of presses is irrelevant. Collecting the effect of each single press into a matrix,

\[ A\in\mathbb{F}_2^{V\times V},\qquad A_{uv}=\begin{cases}1,& u\in N[v],\\ 0,&\text{otherwise,}\end{cases} \qquad\text{equivalently } A=I+\mathrm{Adj}(G), \]

where \(G=(V,E)\) is the board's adjacency graph, pressing the set \(x\) changes the state \(s\) to \(s+Ax\). Note \(A\) is symmetric (adjacency is symmetric) and has an all-ones diagonal (every press toggles its own light).

Starting from all-off (\(s=\mathbf{0}\)) and aiming for all-on (\(s=\mathbf{1}\)) thus amounts to solving

\[ A x=\mathbf{1} \qquad\text{over }\mathbb{F}_2 . \tag{1} \]
Definition 1. The all-on board is reachable (the instance is solvable) iff \(\mathbf{1}\in\operatorname{Im}(A)\). Vectors in \(\ker(A)\) are called quiet patterns: pressing such a set changes nothing.

Solvability is governed by a Fredholm-type alternative that we will use repeatedly.

Lemma 2 (Solvability criterion). For any \(A\in\mathbb{F}_2^{m\times m}\) and the standard form \(\langle u,v\rangle=u^{\top}v\), \[ \operatorname{Im}(A)=\bigl(\ker(A^{\top})\bigr)^{\perp} =\{\,y:\langle k,y\rangle=0\ \text{for all }k\in\ker(A^{\top})\,\}. \] In particular, if \(A\) is symmetric then \(Ax=b\) is solvable iff \(b\) is orthogonal to every quiet pattern.
Proof. If \(y=Ax\) and \(A^{\top}k=0\), then \(\langle k,y\rangle=k^{\top}Ax=(A^{\top}k)^{\top}x=0\), so \(\operatorname{Im}(A)\subseteq(\ker A^{\top})^{\perp}\). Equality follows by dimension: over any field \(\dim W+\dim W^{\perp}=m\), hence \(\dim(\ker A^{\top})^{\perp}=m-\dim\ker A^{\top}=\operatorname{rank}A^{\top} =\operatorname{rank}A=\dim\operatorname{Im}(A)\). A subspace contained in another of the same dimension equals it. (The argument is valid over \(\mathbb{F}_2\), even though there \(W\) may meet \(W^{\perp}\).) □
Remark 3. When (1) is solvable it has exactly \(|\ker A|=2^{\dim\ker A}\) solutions, and \(A\) is invertible exactly when every target is reachable by a unique strategy. The quiet patterns are the only obstruction to solvability.

2Six topologies

We keep the cross-shaped button but change the board's adjacency by identifying the edges of the fundamental square, producing six surfaces. Throughout, the board is the \(n\times n\) array of cells, indexed by \((r,c)\) with \(0\le r,c\le n-1\); the topology specifies how the four orthogonal neighbors are computed near the boundary. Each identification we use is an involution, so the resulting adjacency graph \(G\) is simple and symmetric.

  • Square: no identification; the four sides are free boundary.
  • Cylinder: the left and right edges are glued directly (columns wrap, \(c\mapsto c\bmod n\)); top and bottom remain boundary.
  • Möbius strip: left and right glued with a flip—crossing the seam sends \((r,n)\mapsto(n{-}1{-}r,0)\); top and bottom remain boundary.
  • Torus: both pairs glued directly; every neighbor wraps, \((r,c)\) has degree \(4\).
  • Klein bottle: top and bottom glued directly, left and right glued with a flip; both directions wrap, degree \(4\).
  • Projective plane: both pairs glued with a flip (the antipodal gluing \(abab\)); both directions wrap.

The point of Figure 1 for us is modest: whatever the gluing, the board is still a finite simple graph in which a button toggles itself and its neighbors. That is all the next section needs.

3Every topology is solvable

We prove that the all-on board is always reachable from the all-off board—on all six surfaces, at every size. The heart of the matter is a one-line identity in characteristic two.

Lemma 4 (Diagonal lemma). Let \(A\in\mathbb{F}_2^{m\times m}\) be symmetric. Then for every \(k\in\mathbb{F}_2^{m}\), \[ k^{\top}Ak=\sum_{i}A_{ii}\,k_i=\langle\operatorname{diag}(A),k\rangle. \]
Proof. Expand \(k^{\top}Ak=\sum_{i,j}A_{ij}k_ik_j\). The off-diagonal part is \(\sum_{i<j}(A_{ij}+A_{ji})k_ik_j=\sum_{i<j}2A_{ij}k_ik_j=0\) in \(\mathbb{F}_2\), using \(A_{ij}=A_{ji}\). The diagonal part is \(\sum_i A_{ii}k_i^2=\sum_i A_{ii}k_i\) since \(k_i^2=k_i\). □
Corollary 5. If \(A\) is symmetric with \(\operatorname{diag}(A)=\mathbf{1}\), then every quiet pattern \(k\in\ker(A)\) has an even number of \(1\)'s, i.e.\ \(\langle\mathbf{1},k\rangle=0\).
Proof. Take \(k\in\ker A\). By Lemma 4, \(\langle\mathbf{1},k\rangle=\langle\operatorname{diag}A,k\rangle=k^{\top}Ak=k^{\top}(Ak) =k^{\top}\mathbf{0}=0.\) □
Theorem 6 (Sutner). Let \(G\) be any finite simple graph and \(A=I+\mathrm{Adj}(G)\) its closed-neighborhood matrix over \(\mathbb{F}_2\). Then \(Ax=\mathbf{1}\) is solvable: the all-on configuration is always reachable from all-off.
Proof. \(A\) is symmetric (adjacency is symmetric) and has \(\operatorname{diag}(A)=\mathbf{1}\) (each vertex lies in its own closed neighborhood). By Corollary 5 every quiet pattern is orthogonal to \(\mathbf{1}\), so \(\mathbf{1}\in(\ker A)^{\perp}=\operatorname{Im}(A)\) by Lemma 2. □
Corollary 7. On each of the six boards of Section 2, for every \(n\), the all-on board can be reached from the all-off board.
Proof. Every topology defines a finite simple graph \(G\) on the \(n^{2}\) cells; the only thing to check is that the twisted seams of the Möbius strip, Klein bottle, and projective plane still give a symmetric adjacency, which holds because each seam map is an involution: e.g.\ on the Möbius strip the right-neighbor rule \((r,n)\mapsto(n{-}1{-}r,0)\) has inverse \((n{-}1{-}r,0)\mapsto(r,n)\), so \(u\sim v\) iff \(v\sim u\). Theorem 6 then applies. □
Remark 8 (An explicit witness). Pressing every button gives \(A\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{1}+\mathrm{Adj}(G)\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{1}+(\deg \bmod 2)\). So “press everything” is itself a solution exactly when every closed neighborhood has odd size, i.e.\ every vertex has even degree. This holds on the torus and the Klein bottle (degree \(4\) everywhere, \(n\ge3\)), where the all-on board is cleared by a single sweep. On the square, cylinder, Möbius strip, and projective plane some cells have odd degree, so “press everything” fails—yet Theorem 6 still guarantees a (less obvious) solution.
Remark 9. Nothing in the proof used the grid: Theorem 6 is Sutner's classical result that the all-ones problem is solvable on any graph. Topology is therefore irrelevant to feasibility; it governs only what lies downstream—the dimension \(\dim\ker A\), hence the number of solutions \(2^{\dim\ker A}\) and whether the solution is unique.

5Arbitrary stamps

Now replace the cross by an arbitrary stamp: a finite set \(S\subseteq\mathbb{Z}^{2}\) of offsets. Pressing the button at cell \(p\) toggles every light \(p+s\) for \(s\in S\) (read through the topology's identifications, and dropping offsets that fall off a free boundary). The toggle matrix becomes

\[ A_{uv}=\mathbf{1}\bigl[\,u-v\in S\,\bigr] \]

on a boundaryless board, and a truncated version of this near free edges. The cross is the stamp \(S=\{(0,0),(\pm1,0),(0,\pm1)\}\). We assume the stamp is smaller than the board, so on a boundaryless surface it never wraps onto itself.

The right invariants are the row and column weights of \(A\):

\[ (A\mathbf{1})_u=\#\{\text{buttons toggling light }u\},\qquad (A^{\top}\mathbf{1})_v=\#\{\text{lights toggled by button }v\}. \]
Lemma 10. Let \(A\in\mathbb{F}_2^{m\times m}\) with \(m=n^{2}\).
  • (A) If \(A\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{1}\) (every light is toggled by an odd number of buttons), then \(Ax=\mathbf{1}\) is solvable.
  • (B) If \(A^{\top}\mathbf{1}=\mathbf{0}\) (every button toggles an even number of lights), then \(\operatorname{Im}(A)\) contains only even-weight vectors; hence \(Ax=\mathbf{1}\) is unsolvable whenever \(n^{2}\) is odd.
Proof. (A) For \(k\in\ker(A^{\top})\), \(\langle\mathbf{1},k\rangle=\langle A\mathbf{1},k\rangle=\mathbf{1}^{\top}A^{\top}k =\mathbf{1}^{\top}\mathbf{0}=0\), so \(\mathbf{1}\perp\ker(A^{\top})\) and Lemma 2 gives \(\mathbf{1}\in\operatorname{Im}(A)\). (B) If \(\mathbf{1}=Ax\) then \(\langle\mathbf{1},\mathbf{1}\rangle=\langle\mathbf{1},Ax\rangle =\langle A^{\top}\mathbf{1},x\rangle=0\), while \(\langle\mathbf{1},\mathbf{1}\rangle=n^{2}\bmod 2\); so \(n^{2}\) must be even. □

Boundaryless surfaces: the parity dichotomy

On the torus and the Klein bottle there is no free boundary, and a stamp that fits hits each light by exactly \(|S|\) buttons and each button hits exactly \(|S|\) lights: \(A\mathbf{1}=A^{\top}\mathbf{1}=(|S|\bmod 2)\,\mathbf{1}\). Lemma 10 then gives a clean dichotomy.

Theorem 11. On the torus and on the Klein bottle, for a stamp \(S\) that fits inside the board: \[ |S|\ \text{odd}\ \Longrightarrow\ \text{solvable for every }n;\qquad |S|\ \text{even}\ \Longrightarrow\ \text{unsolvable for every odd }n. \]

The Klein bottle's twist only permutes which buttons hit a light, never how many, so it behaves exactly like the torus. (For even \(|S|\) on an even board the size-parity obstruction vanishes and solvability depends on finer arithmetic of \(S\).)

Remark 12 (The augmentation map). Readers fluent in group algebras will recognize the proof of Theorem 11 as the augmentation argument with its scaffolding removed. Identify \(\mathbb{F}_2^{\mathbb{Z}_n^2}\) with \(R=\mathbb{F}_2[\mathbb{Z}_n^2]\cong\mathbb{F}_2[x,y]/(x^n{-}1,y^n{-}1)\); then \(A\) is multiplication by the stamp polynomial \(s=\sum_{(a,b)\in S}x^a y^b\), the all-ones target is \(u=\sum_g g\), and the functional \(\langle\mathbf{1},\cdot\rangle\) is precisely the augmentation homomorphism \(\varepsilon\colon R\to\mathbb{F}_2\), \(\varepsilon(\sum c_g g)=\sum c_g\), with \(\varepsilon(s)=|S|\bmod 2\). The constancy of the row/column sums used above is nothing but the homomorphism identity \(\varepsilon(sf)=\varepsilon(s)\varepsilon(f)\). Lemma 10(A,B) thus already contain the augmentation argument and are strictly more elementary, needing only \(\langle\mathbf{1},Ax\rangle=\langle A^{\top}\mathbf{1},x\rangle\); we keep that form.

Bounded surfaces: truncation breaks the rule

On the square, cylinder, and Möbius strip, offsets falling off a free edge are dropped, so boundary row/column weights sink below \(|S|\) and their parities are no longer controlled by \(|S|\). Lemma 10 still applies—to the truncated weights—but the size-parity statement of Theorem 11 fails in general. What survives unconditionally is the symmetric special case, which is exactly Theorem 6 applied to the stamp's own graph and is robust to truncation (clipping preserves both symmetry and the unit diagonal):

Proposition 13. If the stamp is centrally symmetric, \(S=-S\), and contains its center, \(0\in S\), then \(Ax=\mathbf{1}\) is solvable on every topology, for every \(n\). (Such a stamp automatically has odd size in \(\mathbb{Z}^{2}\), since its non-central cells pair off as \(\{s,-s\}\).)

Table 1 reports exact solvability (by Gaussian elimination over \(\mathbb{F}_2\)) for several stamps across all five flat topologies and \(n=3,\dots,7\); each entry is a string of Y/N for those five sizes.

Three lessons read off the table:

  • The odd-size guarantee is local to the boundaryless surfaces. The asymmetric L-tromino (\(|S|=3\)) is unsolvable on the cylinder for every \(n\) and size-dependent on the Möbius strip, because truncation turns a boundary light's toggle-count even and defeats Lemma 10(A).
  • So is the even-size obstruction. The vertical domino (\(|S|=2\)) becomes solvable on the square, cylinder, and Möbius strip: a bottom-row button loses its lower cell and toggles just one light (odd), so columns are no longer all even and Lemma 10(B) no longer bites.
  • But parity can survive truncation. The \(2\times2\) block (\(|S|=4\)) keeps every column even even after clipping (a boundary button loses an even chunk), so the cylinder and Möbius strip still inherit the torus dichotomy.

The unifying objects are the parity vectors \(A\mathbf{1}\) and \(A^{\top}\mathbf{1}\); the quantity \(|S|\bmod 2\) is merely their boundaryless shadow. The cross owes its universal solvability not to its size but to being centrally symmetric and containing its center (Proposition 13)—which is why the horizontal and vertical trominoes, also symmetric-with-center, succeed everywhere, while the merely odd-sized L-tromino does not.

Summary

For the cross specifically—and indeed for any centrally symmetric stamp containing its center—all six surfaces are solvable for every size, by the single diagonal-lemma argument of Theorem 6.
